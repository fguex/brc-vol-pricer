01 BRC Product Definition & Payoff

A Barrier Reverse Convertible (BRC) is a yield-enhancement structured product. The investor receives a fixed coupon and the full nominal at maturity — unless the underlying asset breaches a down-and-in barrier during the product life.

Payoff at maturity T

\[ \text{Payoff} = \begin{cases} N \cdot (1 + c) & \text{if } \min_{t \in [0,T]} S_t > B \quad \text{(barrier never touched)} \\ N \cdot (1 + c) & \text{if barrier touched AND } S_T \geq S_0 \quad \text{(recovery)} \\ N \cdot \dfrac{S_T}{S_0} \cdot (1 + c) & \text{if barrier touched AND } S_T < S_0 \quad \text{(capital loss)} \end{cases} \]

Where \(N\) is notional, \(c\) is the coupon rate, \(B = S_0 \cdot b\) with \(b \in (0,1)\) the barrier level (e.g. 60%), and \(S_t\) the underlying spot price.

Equivalent decomposition

\[ \text{BRC} = \underbrace{ZCB}_{\text{zero-coupon bond}} + \underbrace{\text{coupon}}_{\text{fixed income}} - \underbrace{\text{DIPoP}(K=S_0, B)}_{\text{down-and-in put, knocked in at barrier}} \]
Key intuition: The investor is short a down-and-in put. The coupon compensates for this risk. When vol is high, the DIPoP is more expensive → higher coupon offered to investors.

Barrier monitoring

In practice, barriers are monitored discretely (daily closing prices), not continuously. This has significant pricing implications handled by the BGK correction (Section 5).

02 Mathematical Foundations

Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)

The underlying follows risk-neutral GBM:

\[ dS_t = r S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t \]

Under the risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(r\) is the risk-free rate and \(W_t\) is a standard Brownian motion. The exact solution:

\[ S_t = S_0 \exp\!\left[\left(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t\right] \]

Discrete simulation

Simulating daily steps \(\Delta t = 1/252\):

\[ S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \exp\!\left[\left(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z\right], \quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1) \]

Price as discounted expectation

\[ V_0 = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[\text{Payoff}(S_0, S_1, \ldots, S_T)\right] \]

Monte Carlo approximates this expectation over \(N_{sim}\) paths:

\[ \hat{V}_0 = e^{-rT} \cdot \frac{1}{N_{sim}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{sim}} \text{Payoff}^{(i)} \]
Standard error: \(\text{SE} = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_{payoff}}{\sqrt{N_{sim}}}\). To halve the error, quadruple the number of paths.

03 Volatility Surface

Implied vs realised volatility

Black-Scholes assumes constant \(\sigma\). In practice, implied volatility varies with strike \(K\) and maturity \(T\): this is the volatility surface \(\sigma_{imp}(K, T)\).

Moneyness parametrisation

We use log-moneyness \(m = \ln(K/F)\) where \(F = S_0 e^{rT}\) is the forward. A quadratic smile:

\[ \sigma_{imp}(m) = \sigma_{ATM} + \alpha \cdot m + \beta \cdot m^2 \]
ParameterEffectSign for equities
\(\sigma_{ATM}\)At-the-money levelpositive
\(\alpha\) (skew)Asymmetry — puts more expensive than callsnegative (left skew)
\(\beta\) (curvature)Wings more expensive — smile shapepositive
\[ \sigma_{barrier} = \sigma_{imp}\!\left(m = \ln\!\frac{B}{F}\right) \]
Important: The barrier is typically deep OTM (60–70% of spot), in the left tail. The skew strongly increases \(\sigma\) there. Using flat vol underestimates the BRC price (DIPoP is underpriced).

Surface construction from market data

Using yfinance option chains, we extract implied vols at available strikes and maturities, then fit the quadratic parametrisation by minimising:

\[ \min_{\sigma_{ATM}, \alpha, \beta} \sum_i \left(\sigma_{imp}^{market}(K_i) - \sigma_{imp}^{model}(m_i)\right)^2 \]

04 Monte Carlo Engine

Algorithm overview

For each path \(i = 1, \ldots, N_{sim}\):

  1. Draw \(N_{days}\) standard normals \(Z_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\)
  2. Simulate path \(S_0, S_{\Delta t}, \ldots, S_T\)
  3. Check barrier: \(\text{knocked} = \mathbf{1}\left[\min_j S_j \leq B_{adj}\right]\) where \(B_{adj}\) is the BGK-adjusted barrier
  4. Compute payoff according to terminal condition

Average and discount: \(\hat{V}_0 = e^{-rT} \cdot \text{mean(payoffs)}\)

Confidence interval

\[ \hat{V}_0 \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{\text{std(payoffs)}}{\sqrt{N_{sim}}} \]

05 BGK Continuity Correction

Broadie, Glasserman & Kou (1997) showed that a discrete barrier \(B\) is equivalent to a continuous barrier \(B'\):

\[ B_{adj} = B \cdot e^{\pm\, \beta^* \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}} \]

where \(\beta^* = \dfrac{\zeta(1/2)}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx 0.5826\), \(\Delta t = 1/252\) (daily monitoring).

  • Down barrier: \(B_{adj} = B \cdot e^{-\beta^* \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}\) — adjust barrier down
  • Up barrier: \(B_{adj} = B \cdot e^{+\beta^* \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}\) — adjust barrier up
Intuition: Discrete monitoring misses some crossings that continuous monitoring would catch. The correction shifts the barrier so that the discrete-monitoring price matches the continuous-monitoring closed form. For BRC (down barrier), the adjusted barrier is slightly lower — meaning barrier is slightly harder to breach, increasing the product price.
\[ \Delta \text{Price}_{BGK} = V_0^{discrete} - V_0^{continuous} \approx \frac{\partial V}{\partial B} \cdot B \cdot \beta^* \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \]

06 Variance Reduction

Antithetic variates

For each random draw \(Z\), also simulate with \(-Z\). The payoff estimator becomes:

\[ \hat{V}^{AV} = \frac{1}{2}\left(V(Z) + V(-Z)\right) \]

This halves the number of independent random draws needed. Effective when payoff is monotone in \(Z\).

Common Random Numbers (CRN)

When comparing two prices (flat vol vs skew), use the same random paths for both. This reduces variance of the difference:

\[ \text{Var}\!\left[\hat{V}^{skew} - \hat{V}^{flat}\right] = \text{Var}\!\left[\hat{V}^{skew}\right] + \text{Var}\!\left[\hat{V}^{flat}\right] - 2\,\text{Cov}\!\left[\hat{V}^{skew}, \hat{V}^{flat}\right] \]

With CRN, the covariance term is large and positive, dramatically reducing variance of the price difference.

10 Hero Feature: Flat vs Skew Comparison

The central insight of this project — and what makes it directly relevant for a structuring desk:

The question: By how much (in basis points) does ignoring the volatility skew underestimate the fair value of a BRC?

What happens when skew is ignored

The barrier is deep OTM (e.g. 60% of spot). On the left tail of the smile, implied vol is significantly higher than ATM vol. The DIPoP embedded in the BRC is therefore underpriced if flat vol is used:

\[ V_{BRC}^{skew} - V_{BRC}^{flat} \approx \frac{\partial V_{BRC}}{\partial \sigma}\bigg|_{\sigma=\sigma_{flat}} \cdot \left(\sigma_{skew} - \sigma_{flat}\right) > 0 \]

In practice this difference can be 50–200 basis points for a 1-year BRC with 60% barrier on a volatile single stock.
